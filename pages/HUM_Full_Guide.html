CONTRACT ACT, 1872 — Q1 (No Option)
CONTRACT

What is a Contract? Essential Elements

Definition and Formation Stages

"All contracts are agreements, but all agreements are not contracts." — An agreement only becomes a contract when it is legally enforceable.

Formation follows four stages: Proposal (Offer) → Acceptance (Promise) → Agreement → Contract

8 Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

1. Offer & AcceptanceClear offer by one party, absolute and unconditional acceptance by the other.
2. Intention to Create Legal RelationsParties must intend legal consequences. Social/domestic agreements generally excluded.
3. Lawful ConsiderationSomething of value given by each party. "No consideration, no contract." Can be past, present, or future.
4. Capacity of PartiesMust be adult (≥18), of sound mind, not disqualified by law. Minors cannot contract.
5. Free ConsentNo coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake.
6. Legality of ObjectPurpose must be legal. Contracts for illegal acts are void.
7. Certainty & PossibilityTerms must be clear and certain. Act must be physically and legally possible.
8. FormalitiesAny required writing, stamp duty, or registration must be completed.
If any ONE of the 8 elements is missing, there is no valid contract.

Proposal (Offer)

  • Complete when: Communicated to the person it is made to (e.g., offer letter received)
  • Revocation: An offer can be revoked any time before acceptance is communicated. Revocation is complete against proposer when sent; against offeree when received.
  • Specific Offer: Made to a specific person or group
  • General Offer: Made to the public at large (e.g., reward posters)

Acceptance

  • For proposer: Acceptance is complete the moment it is sent (posted) — cannot be stopped
  • For acceptor: Complete only when the proposer actually receives the acceptance
  • Revocation by acceptor: Possible before communication of acceptance is complete against the acceptor (before proposer receives it)
  • Acceptance must be absolute and unqualified. Conditional acceptance = counter-offer.

Consideration

Consideration = the "price of a promise" — an act, abstinence, or promise done at the desire of the promisor.

  • Must move at the desire of the promisor
  • Can be past, present, or future
  • Need not be adequate (fair value), but must be real
  • No consideration = void agreement ("No consideration, no contract")

Exceptions — Valid Contracts Without Consideration

  1. Agreements based on natural love and affection (written + registered, near relations)
  2. Promise to compensate for something voluntarily done
  3. Promise to pay a debt barred by limitation law

Void, Voidable, Unenforceable & Illegal Agreements

TypeMeaningExample

Performance and Termination of Contracts

Parties must perform or offer to perform their promises unless excused by law. A contract ends by:

  • By Performance: Both parties fulfill obligations
  • By Mutual Consent: Parties agree to cancel or alter
  • By Supervening Impossibility: Unforeseen event makes performance impossible (destruction of subject matter, change in law)
  • By Lapse of Time: Not performed within specified/reasonable time
  • By Operation of Law: Death, insolvency, or merger
  • By Breach of Contract: One party fails to perform

Breach of Contract

Breach = one party fails to perform, refuses to perform, or performs improperly without lawful excuse.

Anticipatory Breach
  • Party declares in advance they will not perform, before the due date
  • Mode 1: Express refusal — "I will not deliver the chairs"
  • Mode 2: Implied repudiation — makes performance impossible by actions (e.g., selling goods promised to buyer to a third party)
  • Innocent party may sue immediately or wait until due date
Actual Breach
  • Breach happens at or during the time of performance
  • At due date: fails to perform on the agreed date
  • During performance: starts but stops midway or performs improperly
  • Gives rise to remedies immediately

AGENCY

The Law of Agency

Definition and Creation

An "agent" is a person employed to do any act for another or to represent another in dealings with third persons. The person for whom the act is done is the "principal."
  • Any adult of sound mind can appoint an agent
  • Any person can be an agent (even minors — but cannot be held liable to principal)
  • No consideration necessary to create agency
  • Authority can be express (clearly stated) or implied (from conduct/circumstances)
  • Created by: express/implied agreement, ratification, estoppel, or necessity

Agent's Authority and Duties

Agent's Duties to Principal
  • Conduct business per principal's instructions; if none — per custom of trade
  • Act with reasonable skill and diligence
  • Render proper accounts
  • Communicate with the principal
  • Not to delegate (unless authorized — sub-agents)
  • Pay over all money received on principal's behalf
Principal's Duties to Agent
  • Indemnify agent for all lawful acts done in good faith
  • Pay agreed remuneration
  • No remuneration if agent guilty of misconduct
  • Compensate agent for injury caused by employer's negligence
  • No protection for agents doing illegal acts (e.g., bribery)
In an emergency, an agent can do anything necessary to protect the principal from loss — like any careful person would do for themselves.

Sub-agents vs Substitutes

  • Sub-agent: Agent hires someone under them. Principal is responsible to third parties for sub-agent's acts. Agent is responsible to principal for sub-agent's acts. Sub-agent is NOT directly responsible to principal (unless fraud/intentional wrongdoing).
  • Substitute (co-agent): When agent has authority to appoint another, that person becomes a direct agent of the principal — NOT a sub-agent.

Termination of Agency

Agency is terminated by:

  • Principal revoking the agent's authority
  • Agent renouncing the business
  • Completion of the business
  • Death or insanity of either party
  • Principal declared bankrupt/insolvent

When principal dies or becomes insane, agent must take all reasonable steps to protect and preserve the business/property on behalf of legal heirs.

INDEMNITY & GUARANTEE

Indemnity, Guarantee

Contract of Indemnity

A contract where one party (indemnifier) promises to save the other (indemnity-holder) from loss caused by the conduct of the promisor or any other person.
  • Parties: Indemnifier (promises to compensate) and Indemnity-holder (to be protected)
  • Example: Insurance contracts (except life insurance) are contracts of indemnity
  • Express indemnity: Explicitly promised in contract
  • Implied indemnity: Inferred from conduct/circumstances (e.g., employer must indemnify employee who follows employer's instructions and causes accidental harm)

Contract of Guarantee

A contract to perform the promise or discharge the liability of a third person (principal debtor) in case of their default.
  • Parties: Surety/Guarantor (X), Principal Debtor (Y), Creditor (Z)
  • Surety's liability is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor
  • A guarantee is a tripartite agreement — three contracts in one:
    1. Contract between Creditor and Principal Debtor (main debt)
    2. Contract between Creditor and Surety (guarantee promise)
    3. Implied contract between Principal Debtor and Surety (debtor will indemnify surety if surety pays)
Specific Guarantee
  • For a single specific transaction
  • Ends automatically when that transaction is completed
  • Example: B guarantees A's one-time loan of 100,000 Tk
  • Once loan is repaid, B's liability ends completely
Continuing Guarantee
  • Extends to a series of transactions
  • Remains in force until revoked
  • Example: B guarantees all goods supplied to A on credit up to 500,000 Tk
  • Liability continues until limit reached or guarantee revoked
BAILMENT & PLEDGE

Bailment and Pledge

Bailment

The temporary transfer of goods from one party (Bailor) to another (Bailee) under a contract, with the condition that goods will be returned or handled as directed once the purpose is fulfilled.
  • Example: Leaving your car at a garage for repair
  • Bailee must take care of the goods as a person of ordinary prudence would
  • Bailee must return goods when purpose is accomplished

Pledge

A special type of bailment where goods are delivered as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise.
  • Pledgor: Person who delivers goods (borrower)
  • Pledgee: Person to whom goods are delivered (lender)
  • Example: Pledging jewelry with a lender to secure a loan
  • Pledgee can sell pledged goods if pledgor defaults — after reasonable notice
Bailment vs Pledge
  • Both involve transfer of goods
  • Bailment: for safekeeping, repair, transport, or use
  • Pledge: specifically as security for a debt
  • Pledge: pledgee can sell on default; bailee cannot
Indemnity vs Guarantee
  • Indemnity: 2 parties — indemnifier protects indemnity-holder from loss
  • Guarantee: 3 parties — surety guarantees debtor's obligation to creditor
  • Indemnity: primary liability of indemnifier
  • Guarantee: surety's liability is secondary (arises only on debtor's default)

COMPANY ACT

Formation, Incorporation & Documents

Formation of a Company

TypeMinimum PeopleLiabilityUse
Public Company7 or moreLimited by shares (most common)Large businesses, listed on stock exchange
Private Company2 or moreLimited by shares or guaranteeSmall businesses, family companies
Limited by Shares—Only unpaid amount on sharesStartups, commercial companies
Limited by Guarantee—Fixed agreed amount on winding upNon-profits, NGOs, clubs
Unlimited Company—No limit — personal assets at riskVery rare today

Memorandum of Association (MoA)

The MoA is the constitution of the company — defines its relationship with the outside world. Requirements for company limited by shares:

  1. Name of company (must end with "Limited")
  2. Registered office address
  3. Objectives (purpose of the company)
  4. Statement that member liability is limited
  5. Amount of share capital and division into shares
  • Must be printed, divided into numbered paragraphs, signed by each subscriber in front of two attesting witnesses
  • Alteration of objects clause: requires special resolution + court consent
  • Permitted reasons for alteration: efficiency, new/improved methods, expanding area, fitting new business, restricting objectives, disposing of business, amalgamation

Articles of Association (AoA)

  • Internal rulebook — regulates day-to-day management
  • Mandatory for: companies limited by guarantee, unlimited companies
  • Optional for: companies limited by shares
  • Same formalities as MoA: printed, numbered, signed before witnesses

Company Name Rules

  • Cannot use same or deceptively similar name as existing company
  • If accidentally registered with conflicting name → Registrar can order change within 120 days
  • Non-compliance fines: Company = Tk 500/day; Officers = Tk 100/day
  • Cannot use names of UN, WHO, or similar international organizations without permission
  • Change of name: requires special resolution + Registrar's written approval; then new certificate issued
  • Name must appear on: office frontage, company seal, all official documents and correspondence

Shares and Share Capital

Nature of SharesShares are movable property. Each share distinguished by a unique number. Easily sold, gifted, or inherited.
Alteration of Share CapitalCompany can: increase, consolidate, convert to stock, subdivide, or cancel unissued shares — by passing a resolution.
Share Premium AccountIf shares issued above face value, the excess is share premium — kept in a special account, treated like paid-up capital. Used for: bonus shares, writing off startup costs, redemption premium.
Own Share RestrictionCompany CANNOT buy its own shares or give loans/guarantees for buying its own shares (except through legal capital reduction process).
Reduction of Share CapitalCan cancel unpaid liability, write off lost capital, or return excess capital — with court approval + memorandum update.
Member DefinitionEvery subscriber of MoA is automatically a member. Others become members when their name enters the register of members.

Meetings

Meeting TypeWhenNotice RequiredKey Rules
Annual General Meeting (AGM)Once a year; not more than 15 months apart14 days' written noticeFirst AGM within 18 months of incorporation. Penalty for default: Tk 10,000 + Tk 250/day
Statutory MeetingOnce, within 1–6 months of starting business21 days (report sent to members)Only public companies. Directors prepare Statutory Report; certified by 2 directors + auditors. Not applicable to private companies.

Resolutions

Ordinary Resolution
  • Simple majority (more than 50%)
  • Routine business decisions
  • e.g., appointing replacement director after removal
Extraordinary / Special Resolution
  • At least 75% majority (3 out of 4 members)
  • Special resolution: requires 21 days' notice
  • Used for: major changes (altering AoA, winding up, capital reduction)
DIRECTORS

Directors — Appointment, Qualifications, Removal

Number of Directors

  • Public companies (and private subsidiaries of public companies): minimum 3 directors
  • Other private companies: minimum 2 directors
  • Only natural persons (human beings) can be directors

Appointment, Qualification & Disqualification

Appointment
  • First directors = subscribers of MoA (act until official directors appointed)
  • Elected by members at general meeting from among members
  • In public companies: at least 1/3 of directors must be rotatable (can be retired and re-elected)
Disqualifications
  • Declared of unsound mind by court
  • Undischarged insolvent
  • Insolvency petition pending
  • Call on shares unpaid for 6+ months
  • Minor (under 18)
  • Company may add extra disqualifications via articles

Qualification Shares

Every director must hold a specified number of shares (qualification shares) as stated in the articles. If not already held, must be acquired within 60 days of appointment.

Removal & Managing Director Rules

  • Removal: By extraordinary resolution (75% vote) before term expires. Removed director cannot be re-appointed by the Board. Replacement appointed by ordinary resolution.
  • Loans to Directors: Company cannot give loans/guarantees to its own directors, their firms, or their associated companies. Exception: if board approves, general meeting approves, disclosed in balance sheet. Even then: total loan ≤ 50% of director's own shares.
  • Managing Director restrictions: Cannot be MD of more than one company (without government approval). Cannot appoint without general meeting approval. Maximum appointment period: 5 years at a time.

Prospectus and Auditors

  • Prospectus: Formal legal document when offering shares to public. Must include: business activities, securities offered, purpose of funds, management details, risk factors. Penalty for untrue statements: imprisonment up to 2 years, or fine up to Tk 5,000, or both.
  • Auditors: Appointed at every AGM to serve until next AGM. Company informs auditor within 7 days; auditor tells Registrar within 30 days of acceptance/refusal. Government can also appoint auditors.
  • Registered Office: Must be established from day of starting business or within 28 days of incorporation. Notify Registrar within 28 days of any address change. Penalty: Tk 200/day.
WINDING UP

Winding Up of a Company

Three Modes of Winding Up

1. By the CourtCourt orders company to close — for non-payment of debts, statutory failures, inactivity, few members, or if just and equitable.
2. VoluntarilyCompany itself decides to close — by passing a special resolution or extraordinary resolution when debts make continuation impossible.
3. Under Court SupervisionCompany starts voluntary winding up, but court monitors to ensure proper process.

Grounds for Winding Up by Court (Section 241)

  1. Company passes special resolution to be wound up by court
  2. Failed to file statutory report or hold statutory meeting
  3. Doesn't start business within 1 year of formation, or stops business for 1 year
  4. Members fall below 2 (private) or 7 (public company)
  5. Cannot pay its debts
  6. Just and equitable (fraud, mismanagement, deadlock)

Company Unable to Pay Debts

  • Creditor owed more than Tk 500 sends formal written demand to registered office
  • Company fails to pay or arrange payment within 3 weeks → deemed unable to pay
  • OR: court decree to recover debt fails to produce payment

Preferential Payments (Order of Priority in Winding Up)

  1. Taxes, rates, fees owed to government/local authorities (last 12 months)
  2. Salaries of clerks/staff for last 2 months (max Tk 1,000 each)
  3. Wages of laborers/workers for last 2 months (max Tk 500 each)
  4. Workmen's compensation for death/disability
  5. Investigation expenses related to winding up
Preferential debts rank equally among themselves and share proportionally if assets insufficient. They have priority over debenture holders with floating charges.

LABOUR ACT

Workers — Classification, Employment & Termination

Classification of Workers (7 Types)

ApprenticeTrainees learning the job — paid allowances during training period. Not regular employees.
SubstituteTemporary fill-ins for absent permanent or probationary workers.
CasualHired on ad-hoc basis for short, casual tasks. No guarantee of continued work.
TemporaryHired for limited-duration jobs. Work ends when the temporary project/need ends.
ProbationerDoing a permanent job but still in trial period. Clerical: 6 months. Others: 3 months (extendable 3 months for skilled workers).
PermanentHired permanently, or those who successfully complete probation. Even without confirmation letter, become permanent after probation ends.
SeasonalHired only during specific work seasons (e.g., harvest time, sugar mill season).
Service Book: Every employer must provide one free of cost. Employer keeps it safe. Worker gets it back when leaving. Does NOT apply to apprentices, substitutes, or casual workers.

Misconduct, Discipline & Punishment

A worker can be immediately dismissed without notice for: conviction of criminal offence, or serious misconduct.

What is Misconduct?

  • Disobedience of lawful orders
  • Theft, fraud, or dishonesty with employer's property
  • Bribery; long unauthorized absence; habitual late attendance
  • Breaking rules repeatedly; violence, arson, property damage
  • Negligence; falsifying official records

Alternative Punishments (lighter than dismissal)

  • Removal from service; demotion (up to 1 year); stopping promotion (up to 1 year)
  • Withholding salary increment (up to 1 year); fines; suspension without pay (up to 7 days)
  • Censure or warning

Child and Adolescent Employment

  • Strict ban: No child below 14 years can be employed in any job
  • Adolescents (14–18): Can work only with fitness certificate from registered doctor
  • Exceptions: Rules do not apply to apprenticeship or vocational training
  • Parents: Cannot make agreements to employ their child in any work
  • Working hours for adolescents: Max 5 hrs/day and 30 hrs/week (factories/mines); 7 hrs/day and 42 hrs/week (other workplaces)
  • No night work: Cannot work between 7:00 PM and 7:00 AM
  • Max 2 shifts/day; each shift ≤ 7.5 hours; not allowed to work at more than one place per day

Leave Application Procedure

  1. Worker applies in writing, stating address during leave
  2. Employer replies within 7 days of receiving application, or at least 2 days before leave starts (whichever is earlier)
  3. Emergency (leave starting same day or within 3 days): employer replies same day
  4. If approved → leave pass issued. If refused → reason stated before leave date, recorded in register
  5. Extension: worker writes by registered post before current leave ends

Wages — Definition, Payment & Deductions

What Counts as Wages

  • Regular payment from work (basic wages)
  • Bonuses or extra payments promised in contract
  • Payment for leave, holidays, or overtime
  • Payments ordered by court or labour agreements
  • Termination dues (on retirement, resignation, dismissal, etc.)
  • Lay-off or suspension pay

Payment Timing

  • Regular wages: within 7 working days after the end of each wage period
  • After separation (retirement/retrenchment/dismissal/resignation): within 30 working days
  • Must be paid on a working day

Permissible Deductions from Wages

  • Fines (properly imposed); unauthorized absence; damage/loss due to negligence
  • House rent (company housing); approved facilities/services
  • Advance repayments; income tax; court orders; provident fund contributions
  • Cooperative society / insurance; voluntary welfare contributions (written consent)
  • Union subscriptions via check-off system
TRADE UNIONS

Trade Unions and Unfair Labour Practices

Right to Form Trade Unions

  • Both workers and employers can form trade unions
  • Workers can form unions to: negotiate with employers, resolve disputes, join federations, affiliate with international organizations
  • Unions have freedom to make own constitution, elect own leaders, and organize activities independently

Trade Union in Group of Establishments

  • Factories in same area and same industry must form one joint union (not separate unions)
  • A group must have at least 20 workers total
  • Minimum 30% of all workers in the group must be members for registration
  • Non-employees can become union officers — but limited to max 1/4 of total officers
  • Industries that can form group unions regardless of size: road transport, garments (≤100 workers), tea industry, tannery, bidi, handloom, printing press, small hotels, cinema, shipbuilding, construction, rice mills

Unfair Labour Practices

By Employers
  • Forcing workers not to join unions
  • Discriminating in hiring/firing based on union membership
  • Threatening workers for wanting to form/join unions
  • Bribing workers to leave unions (promotions, gifts)
  • Forcing union leaders to sign agreements under threats
  • Interfering in union elections
  • Hiring during legal strikes (without permission)
  • Ignoring Participation Committee recommendations
  • Not replying to official union communications
  • Unfairly transferring union leaders
  • Illegal lock-outs
By Workers
  • Doing union work during working hours without permission
  • Pressuring employers illegally
  • Intimidating workers to join or not join unions
  • Illegal strikes
  • Violence or damage to property during disputes
  • Refusing to comply with settlement agreements
Trade union registration can be cancelled if unfair labour practices prevail.

CH 2

Industrial Hazard

Definition of Hazard

Webster's: "A chance of being harmed or injured, or to expose to harm or danger." Comprehensive: "A hazard is any real potential condition or act that could cause damage to property, harm to environment, disrupt a process operation, or commence injury/death/prolong sickness — but has not yet done so."
Hazard → Accident → Loss of Life and Wealth → Discontinuity/Added Cost → Out of Business

5 Categories of Hazards

Physical HazardsEquipment, machines, electricity, extreme heat/cold, humidity, noise, vibration, moving objects, radiation. Things physically present at the workplace.
Chemical HazardsRaw materials, products, catalyst agents with dangerous properties: explosion, toxicity, corrosion, poisoning, irritation. Examples: acids, dyes, gasoline vapour, H₂S, SO₂, welding fumes.
Biological HazardsMicro-organisms and metabolic products. Examples: bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites from infected animals. Examples: Anthrax, Tuberculosis, HIV, Hepatitis B, Bird Flu.
Psychosocial HazardsFrom working relationships or situational factors causing psychological/mental pressure. Examples: mental stress, monotony of work, fatigue, brain fag, irritation.
Human Factors FailureFrom human faults and lagging. Examples: unsafe work practice, ergonomic errors (forceful movement, improper lifting), poor training, lack of communication, accident proneness.

Hazard Classification by Severity

Probability of Hazard Occurrence

Frequent (10) — likely to occur virtually every time Probable (8) — likely to occur most of the time Occasional (6) — likely to occur sometimes Remote (4) — very unlikely chance of occurring Improbable (2) — no chance of occurrence

Hazard Assessment Formula

Rating Range = Hazard Category Rating × Frequency Rating Rating 100–200: UNACCEPTABLE — Design change/modification is a must Rating 60–90: UNDESIRABLE — Design modification required Rating 20–50: CONDITIONALLY ACCEPTABLE — May be accepted with some change Rating 6–18: ACCEPTABLE — Accepted without review and change

Key Hazard Definitions

TermDefinition
Hazard Rate (H)Rate (occasions/year) at which hazards occur (e.g., pressure in vessel exceeds design pressure)
Protective SystemDevice to prevent hazard occurring (e.g., relief valve, high level trip)
Test Interval (T)Time between successive tests of a protective system
Demand Rate (D)Rate (occasions/year) at which a protective system is called on to act
Failure Rate (F)Rate (occasions/year) at which protective system develops faults
Fractional Dead Time (FDT)Fraction of time that protective system is inactive (probability it fails when needed)
Fractional Dead Time (FDT) = Failure Rate × Test Interval / 2 Hazard Rate = Demand Rate × Fractional Dead Time Example 1: F = 0.1/yr, T = 1 yr, D = 10⁴/yr FDT = 0.1 × 1 / 2 = 0.05 Hazard Rate = 10⁴ × 0.05 = 500/year Example 2: F = 0.5/yr, T = 1 week = 3.5/730 days, D = 1/yr FDT = 3.5 / (2 × 365) = 0.005 (approximately) Hazard Rate = 1 × 0.005 = once in 200 years

Three Types of Adverse Consequences

  • Human Impact: Personal injury, death, disablement, psychological effects
  • Economic Impact: Loss of production, property damage, inventory loss, legal liability, image/goodwill loss
  • Environmental Impact: Air pollution, water pollution, soil pollution

Steps for Hazard Control

  1. Hazard identification
  2. Hazard inventory
  3. Hazard ranking
  4. Assessing probability of occurrence
  5. Hazard rank assessment
  6. Hazard elimination / reduction / control
CH 13

Safety Management

Definitions

Safety of a person: "The minimization of contact between human and hazard — predominantly concerned with prevention of physical harm (injury or death)."
Safety Management: "A concern for safe equipment, process and layout design, safe work methods design, recruitment and retaining of competent employees, promotion of safety awareness, education in safety at all levels, and managerial/financial support for moral and ethical responsibility."

Why Safety? — 6 Reasons

a. Moral/Ethical ObligationEmployers feel moral obligation to safeguard employees from hazards. Employees become integral part of organization.
b. Legal ObligationGovernment laws and acts protect workers. Agencies impose rules, monitor activities, and can fine for unsafe practices.
c. Financial ObligationAccidents incur medical costs, compensation, lost production, equipment damage. Prevention is cheaper than accidents.
d. Corporate ReputationSafe operation ensures stability, peace in organization, and confidence to employees, customers, and stakeholders.
e. Buyer's RequirementSome buyers (e.g., Western buyers for RMG) impose safe work conditions as purchasing requirement.
f. Safety Promotes ProductivitySafety enables: uninterrupted production, high morale, no unplanned shutdowns, better labor-management relations.

6 Activities of Safety Management

  1. Safe Equipment, Process and Layout Design — "Prevention is better than cure." During construction, 4 aspects studied by Project Management Team: plant layout, P&I diagram, equipment/material specs, process chemistry, maintenance/fire protection.
  2. Safe Work Method Design — Safe work procedure for every job designed by specialists per standards, codes, vendor instructions. Supervisors ensure proper procedures are followed. "All technologies will be useless if we do not follow the norms."
  3. Recruiting and Retaining Competent Employees — Experienced manpower is the main asset. Productivity, efficiency, and safety depend on people's capacity and capability.
  4. Promotion of Safety Awareness — "People never plan to fail but usually fail to plan." Management must plan for safety at all levels. Safety committee (safety dept + specialist + workers' representative) for formulating regulations, performing audits.
  5. Education in Safety at All Levels — "People will make mistakes as they are not proper to be infallible." Training, proper selection of workers, educating about hazards — all improve safety management. Behaviour modification improves safety awareness.
  6. Managerial and Financial Support — Basic causes of accidents = poor management policies. Adequate budget allocation essential for effective safety program.

Safety Triangle and Safety Organization

Frank E. Bird Jr. analysis of 1,753,498 accidents — ratio: 1 — Serious or disabling injury 10 — Minor injuries 30 — Property damage accidents 600 — Incidents with no visible injury or damage Lesson: By preventing minor incidents (600), you reduce chance of major accidents (1)

Elements of a Safety Management System (Pyramid)

  • Leadership (top)
  • Resource Allocation
  • Line Responsibility
  • Training, Awareness & Motivation (×2)
  • Continuous Improvement & Practice (base)

Three Essential Elements of Employee Safety System

  • Physical: Safety equipment, tools, machines, facilities, personnel, office
  • Managerial: Safety regulations, training, incentive plan
  • Cultural: Safety practice at all levels, for all

Business Profit vs Safety Investment (Four Stages)

ZoneStageMeaning
AInvestment in Safety is Good BusinessNo safety measures yet. Early investment gives high return — prevents major accidents like plant blowup. Steep improvement in profitability.
BInvestment in Safety is Poor BusinessSome safety in place. Further investment still gives return, but less steep. Some money comes back, not as much as before.
CSafety is Bad Business But Good HumanitySo much spent that company doesn't expect material return. Safety becomes more important than profit. Prevention return doesn't cover investment.
DInvestment Results in Out of BusinessSo much spent on perfect safety that company has forgotten main goal — profit. Organization goes into closure.

PPE TypeTypes/ExamplesKey Notes
Eye ProtectionCover goggles, corrective lenses, protective spectacles, cup goggles, chemical goggles, dust goggles, miners' goggles, welders' gogglesFitted close to eyes; no appreciable distortion; plastic lenses meet optical standards

Leg ProtectionLeggings from ankle to kneeMolten metals/sparks → asbestos or leather; acids/alkalies/hot water → rubber or plastic.

CH 23

Occupational Health Management

Definition (ILO/WHO, 1950)

"Occupational health should aim at: the promotion and maintenance of the highest degree of physical, mental and social well-being of workers in all occupations; the prevention of departures from health caused by working conditions; the protection of workers from risks adverse to health; the placing and maintenance of the worker in an environment adapted to his physiological and psychological equipment."

In short: keep work conditions such that workers do not fall to a legally compensable illness or are out of loss time or free from significant discomfort from working.

6 Aims of Occupational Health

a. Communicable Disease ControlTB, typhoid fever, viral hepatitis, amoebiasis, dengue, malaria, venereal diseases. Adequate immunization programs required.
b. Environmental SanitationSafe drinking water, hygienic food, sanitary toilets, plant cleanliness, sufficient space, proper lighting/aeration/humidity/temperature, housing if possible.
c. Mental HealthPromote happiness, detect emotional stress, provide treatment, rehabilitate those who become ill.
d. Measures for Women & ChildrenAvoid tough/tedious work, restricted working hours, maternity leave and benefits.
e. Health EducationBasic health need. Envisaged at all levels: management, supervisory staff, workers, trade union leaders, community.
f. Family PlanningRecognized as decisive factor for quality of life. Workers must adopt small family norm.

Prevention of Occupational Diseases — 3 Approaches

a. Medical Measures

  • Pre-placement examination: Foundation of efficient occupational health service. Physical/pathological/radiological/ECG/vision tests. Recorded in personal file. May reject/place based on health condition.
  • Periodic examination: Early detection of disease. Once a year typically. Frequency depends on type of occupational exposure.
  • Medical & health care: First aid within factory. Immunization. Company insurance scheme.
  • Notification: Report health-related conditions to national authorities. Initiate prevention measures.
  • Supervision of working environment: Periodic inspection — light, noise, temperature, sanitation, fatigue, shift-work.
  • Maintenance of records: Health records including retired employees (long-term effect hazards).
  • Health education & counseling: Start before employee begins assignment. Charts, posters, organization magazine.

b. Engineering Measures

  • Plant layout & design: proper space, ventilation, lighting, machine guarding
  • Good housekeeping: clean, orderly workspace
  • General ventilation: minimum 5 sq ft opening + 500 cu ft air space per worker
  • Local exhaust ventilation: trap dusts/fumes at source before contaminating workspace
  • Mechanization and substitution: reduce hazard by mechanizing or replacing harmful material with harmless alternative
  • Isolation: separate critical/offensive operations in separate building
  • Protective devices: provide PPE to workers in hazardous environments
  • Statistical monitoring & research: regular review of collected data on health and environmental exposure

c. Statutory/Legislative Measures

Factory laws established in every country to govern industry conditions and safeguard health/welfare. Most important factory laws in Bangladesh compiled as Bangladesh Labour Act 2006, originally from:

  • Factories Act, 1965; Factories Rules, 1979
  • Maternity Benefit Act 1939; Workmen's Compensation Act 1923
  • Children Act, Employment of 1938, Rules 1955

Five Activities of Industrial Medical Services

  1. Health care & emergency medical services: Treatment of sick/injured; first aid; annual checkup; vaccination
  2. Accident prevention: Pre-employment examination; identify hazards; keep sick persons from duty; fitness certificates
  3. Labour welfare: Health education, sanitation maintenance, canteen/facilities inspection
  4. Research & data collection: Accident/sickness data; loss of earning capacity; disease control
